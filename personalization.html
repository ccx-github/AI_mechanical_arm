Personalization and Security Protection

Question: How do you enable users to perform daily activities while collecting accurate data during the process?

Answer

To ensure patients can engage in daily activities while collecting accurate data, our wristband utilizes LeapMotion, released by Leap, to measure hand movements in real-time during the rehabilitation process. As shown in the diagram, LeapMotion is equipped with two grayscale camera sensors and three infrared LED light sources. During use, it can perceive three-dimensional pixels, process vast amounts of pixel data, and identify data such as the user's hand position, movement speed, pointing direction, and joint bending angles, without requiring any additional actions from the user. Simply performing corresponding hand movements within LeapMotion's working range enables functions such as browsing web pages, flipping through photos, reading articles, and playing music on the PC.

Technical Principles

During the early stage of rehabilitation, passive training is necessary because patients are unable to perform active movements. Therefore, we have designed a corresponding exoskeleton control mode.
In the passive rehabilitation mode, when patients lack the strength in their hands or their strength is insufficient to complete a full hand rehabilitation movement, the exoskeleton will drive each finger exoskeleton according to the pre-designed grasping patterns, facilitating the completion of corresponding grasping actions by the hand. The following diagram illustrates the control method structure block diagram of the hand rehabilitation robot in the passive rehabilitation mode.

Diagram of Passive Rehabilitation Training

In the later stage of rehabilitation, patients possess a certain level of muscle strength. By real-time detection of patients' motion intentions during movement and converting them into the system's control targets, the quality of human-computer interaction is improved, enhancing patients' participation and the quality of training. In the active training control method, patients' grasping motion intentions are recognized and motion positions are preset, enabling patients to exercise autonomous control. Simultaneously, the RMS (Root Mean Square) and frequency indicators of forearm muscles are calculated and displayed based on the EMG (Electromyography) window data collected by the MYO armband. This provides visual feedback to patients, allowing them to judge whether they need to rest or continue with their rehabilitation training. Conclusion

The results indicate that passive training has achieved its objectives well. When the active training mode is selected, the rehabilitation exoskeleton can effectively recognize the patient's real-time hand motion intentions and adjust the expected joint angle trajectory of the hand exoskeleton in real-time. This enhances the patient's engagement during the rehabilitation process and improves the human-robot interaction of the rehabilitation robot, which also addresses safety concerns.
